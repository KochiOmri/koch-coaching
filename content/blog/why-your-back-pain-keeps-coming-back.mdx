## The Frustrating Cycle of Back Pain

You've tried stretching. You've done the core exercises your physio gave you. Maybe you've even had injections or considered surgery. Yet the pain keeps coming back — sometimes worse than before.

If this sounds familiar, you're not alone. Back pain affects roughly **80% of people** at some point in their lives, and for many, it becomes a chronic cycle of temporary relief followed by inevitable flare-ups.

The reason? Most treatments address the **symptom** (the pain in your back) without ever fixing the **cause** (the movement dysfunction creating the problem).

## Symptoms vs. Root Cause

Think of your body like a building. If the foundation is uneven, cracks will appear in the walls. You can patch those cracks over and over, but until you fix the foundation, they'll keep appearing.

Your back pain works the same way:

- **The symptom**: Pain, stiffness, or spasm in your lower back
- **The root cause**: A dysfunctional movement pattern somewhere in your kinetic chain — often far from where you feel the pain

Common root causes of recurring back pain include:

### Dysfunctional Gait Mechanics

When you walk, your pelvis should rotate, your thoracic spine should counter-rotate, and your arms should swing in opposition. If any part of this chain isn't working correctly, your lumbar spine absorbs forces it was never designed to handle.

### Hip Immobility

Tight or poorly coordinated hips force your lower back to compensate during everyday movements. Every time you bend, twist, or walk, your spine picks up the slack for what your hips aren't doing.

### Poor Ribcage Position

A flared or depressed ribcage changes how force transfers through your trunk. This alters breathing mechanics, core stability, and spinal loading patterns — all of which can manifest as back pain.

### Foot and Ankle Dysfunction

Your feet are the foundation of your movement. Collapsed arches, limited ankle mobility, or poor foot mechanics send a ripple of compensation up the entire chain that often lands in the lower back.

## Why Conventional Approaches Fall Short

Most conventional treatments for back pain share a common flaw: they focus on the site of pain rather than the source of dysfunction.

| Approach | What It Does | Why It's Not Enough |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Stretching | Temporarily relieves tension | Doesn't address why the muscle is tight |
| Core exercises | Strengthens local muscles | Isolation doesn't fix integration |
| Massage | Releases trigger points | Tension returns because the pattern remains |
| Pain medication | Blocks pain signals | Masks the problem entirely |

## The Biomechanics Approach

A biomechanics-based approach — like Functional Patterns — looks at your body as an integrated system. Instead of asking "where does it hurt?", we ask "**why** does it hurt?"

This means:

1. **Full-body assessment**: Analyzing your standing posture, gait, and movement patterns to find where the chain breaks down
2. **Identifying compensations**: Finding which muscles are overworking and which are underperforming
3. **Corrective training**: Reprogramming dysfunctional patterns through targeted exercises that teach your body to distribute force properly
4. **Progressive loading**: Gradually building your capacity to handle real-world demands without compensating

Breaking the cycle of chronic back pain requires patience and a willingness to look beyond the obvious. It means accepting that the problem might not be in your back at all — and that the solution lies in retraining how your entire body moves.
